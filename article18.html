Fast Fashion

Zainab Rana

November 23rd, 2025

Image from Britannica

We’ve all fallen into the fast fashion trap at some point. You see a cute outfit on TikTok, click a few links, and boom!, It’s on your doorstep before you’ve even thought about it. Brands like Shein, Zara, and Temu are veterans at mass-producing trendy clothes at lightning speed. But behind all the “new arrivals” and aesthetic hauls, a lot of legal drama is starting to unfold(and it’s not as cute as the clothes.)

One of the biggest controversies is over design theft. Small designers spend weeks (sometimes months) creating original pieces. Then, they’ll stumble upon nearly identical versions of their work on a fast fashion site, sold for a fraction of the original price.

In 2023, designer Emma Warren sued Shein, alleging that it had copied her work. The tricky part is that U.S. copyright laws don’t fully protect clothing designs; only prints, logos, or specific artwork are covered. That loophole makes it way too easy for big companies to copy the “vibe” of a design without technically breaking the law. Still, more designers are starting to push back through lawsuits.

Fast fashion isn’t just facing heat for copying styles. A lot of brands rely on overseas factories where working conditions can be rough. There have even been investigations into underpaid or illegally employed workers. California recently passed a law holding brands accountable for wage law violations by their suppliers, which is a significant development. It means companies can’t just turn a blind eye to how their clothes are made.

Another growing issue is the environmental impact. Fast fashion produces clothes at such a fast pace that tons of items end up in landfills after just a few wears. Governments are starting to pass new laws that require brands to be more transparent and up-front about their environmental practices, or they have to pay up. Basically, the “wear it once” culture is catching up to companies legally, too.

Fashion law might not sound like something teenagers need to care about, but it affects creativity, workers’ rights, and the planet we live on. The next time you add something cute to your cart, it’s worth remembering that there might be a bigger story behind that $10 top.
